Rec 1.0 — Customer Master Recipe Batch

Recipe ID: 54576818 Status: Active Author: venkatesh.manohar Generated: 2026-04-01 Version: 15 (by chase.clark)

1. Executive Summary

What it does: This recipe synchronizes customer records from Intercom's Snowflake data warehouse into NetSuite (the company's ERP system). It reads a staging table of customer data prepared by upstream dbt models, splits records by subsidiary (US vs. Ireland), batches them in groups of 100, and calls subsidiary-specific child recipes that upsert customer records into NetSuite.

Why it exists: Intercom operates across multiple legal entities (US and Ireland). Every customer that receives an invoice in NetSuite must first exist as a Customer record in that system. This recipe ensures that all customer data — names, addresses, billing details, Salesforce IDs, and subsidiary assignments — are automatically synchronized from the single source of truth in Snowflake to NetSuite before downstream financial transactions (invoices, credit memos, payments) are processed.

High-level flow: A parent orchestrator recipe (Data Migration Integration CatchUp, ID 54665278) calls this recipe with a date parameter. The recipe queries Snowflake for all customers created on that date, splits them by subsidiary, batches them in groups of 100, and sends each batch to the appropriate NetSuite subsidiary via dedicated child recipes.

2. Business Overview

The Problem

Intercom sells to customers worldwide, but its financial operations run through two legal subsidiaries: Intercom Inc. (US) and Intercom R&D Unlimited (Ireland). Each customer must be set up in NetSuite under the correct subsidiary with accurate billing details before any financial transaction can be processed. Manually creating and updating thousands of customer records would be error-prone and unsustainable at scale.

The Solution

An automated pipeline that:

  1. Reads a curated customer export table from Snowflake (prepared daily by dbt)
  2. Separates customers by their assigned subsidiary (US or IE)
  3. Sends them in efficient batches of 100 to NetSuite for creation or update
  4. Logs every operation for auditability and troubleshooting

3. System Overview

Business View

Workato is the integration platform that reads from Snowflake and writes to NetSuite, handling the orchestration, batching, and error handling.

NetSuite is the ERP/accounting system where customer records must exist before invoices, payments, and credit memos can be created.

Snowflake — Table SHARED_EXTERNAL_WORKATO.NETSUITE_EXPORT_CUSTOMER is a dbt-managed staging table with 28 columns covering identity, billing, and classification fields.

Workato — Recipe function trigger (callable recipe). Uses variables for subsidiary splitting, repeat loops for batching, and recipe function calls for child delegation.

NetSuite — Bulk upsert API via SuiteTalk. Creates/updates Customer records and Customer Subsidiary Relationship records.

4. Architecture Overview

Layered Architecture

Source Layer: Snowflake

Business View

A single, clean table of customer records is prepared daily by automated data pipelines. Each row represents one customer with their billing address, subsidiary assignment, Salesforce link, and status.

Technical View

The table NETSUITE_EXPORT_CUSTOMER is populated by dbt models that join Stripe customer data with Salesforce account data. It includes a CREATED_DATE column used for incremental filtering, and a SUBSIDIARY column with values 'US' or 'IE'.

Orchestration Layer: Workato

Business View

The integration platform reads customer data, intelligently splits it by legal entity, and sends it to NetSuite in manageable chunks to avoid API limits and ensure reliability.

Technical View

The master recipe (54576818) is a callable recipe function triggered by a parent orchestrator (54665278). It uses a double-loop pattern: an outer repeat-while loop for Snowflake pagination (50K rows at a time) and an inner repeat-while loop that slices subsidiary arrays into batches of 100 for NetSuite processing.

Target Layer: NetSuite

Business View

Customer records are created or updated in NetSuite, ready to be referenced by invoices, payments, and other financial transactions.

Technical View

The child recipes use NetSuite's bulk upsert API to create/update Customer records, then a separate bulk create call to establish Customer Subsidiary Relationships. Results are logged to a NetSuite_Integration_Log data mapper for audit trails.

6. End-to-End Flow

Level 0: Simple View

Every day, a scheduling recipe runs and passes a date to the Customer Master Recipe. The recipe queries Snowflake for all customers created on that date, splits them into US and Ireland groups, sends them to NetSuite in batches of 100, and logs the results. At the end, every customer in the export table exists as a valid Customer record in NetSuite, ready to receive invoices and payments.

Level 1: Detailed View

1
Trigger: Parent recipe (Data Migration CatchUp, 54665278) calls Rec 1.0 with a date parameter. This parameter is the target date for which customer records should be synchronized.
2
Extract: Recipe queries SHARED_EXTERNAL_WORKATO.NETSUITE_EXPORT_CUSTOMER in Snowflake, filtering by created_date = '{date}', ordered by external_id, with a limit of 50,000 rows per page.
3
Split: Results are split into two variable arrays — US (where subsidiary = 'US') and IE (where subsidiary = 'IE'). Counts are stored in US_Length and IE_Length.
4
Batch Loop: An inner repeat loop iterates through the arrays in slices of 100 records (index 0..99, 100..199, etc.). For each batch:
5
US Processing: If the US batch slice is present, a list of size US_Batch.length is created, and RF 1.0 Create/Update Customer - US Bulk (54582588) is called with the batch of customer records.
6
IE Processing: If the IE batch slice is present, RF 1.0 Create/Update Customer - IE Bulk (54575580) is called with the IE customer batch.
7
Continue Condition: The batch loop continues as long as either US_Batch or IE_Batch still has records.
8
Pagination: The outer loop re-queries Snowflake only if the previous query returned exactly 50,000 rows (indicating more data exists).

Level 2: Child Recipe Processing (RF 1.0)

Both the US and IE child recipes follow the same pattern:

  1. Receive batch of customer records (up to 100) with fields: ExternalID, CompanyName, Subsidiary, BillingAddress1, BillingCity, BillingState, BillingZipCode, BillingCountry, Email, DefaultBilling, BillingLabel, Terms, DefaultShipping, SFDCID, Status, Category
  2. Upsert Customers in batch via NetSuite bulk API — creates new customers or updates existing ones matched by external ID
  3. Concurrency Wait (custom Ruby code) — pauses to allow NetSuite to process the batch
  4. Create Customer Subsidiary Relationships in bulk — links customers to their subsidiary
  5. Log results to NetSuite_Integration_Log via data mapper, with success/failure details

7. Processing Stages

Stage 1: Ingestion

Business View

The recipe pulls all customer records that were prepared for export on the given date. It retrieves up to 50,000 records at a time, paginating if there are more.

Technical View

A SELECT query against NETSUITE_EXPORT_CUSTOMER with a WHERE created_date = '{date}' filter, ORDER BY external_id, LIMIT 50000. The outer repeat-while loop continues if the result count equals 50,000 (pagination via offset).

Stage 2: Grouping & Classification

Business View

Customers are separated by their legal subsidiary — US or Ireland — because each subsidiary has its own NetSuite configuration and processing rules.

Technical View

The .where(subsidiary: 'US') and .where(subsidiary: 'IE') Workato formula functions filter the row array into two separate variables. Lengths are computed and stored for loop control.

Stage 3: Batching

Business View

Customers are sent to NetSuite in groups of 100. This prevents API overload and ensures that a failure in one batch does not affect the entire run.

Technical View

An inner repeat-while loop slices the subsidiary arrays using index math: array[(100 * index)..(100 * (index + 1) - 1)]. The loop continues while either US_Batch or IE_Batch is present (non-empty slice).

Stage 4: Processing (NetSuite Write)

Business View

Each batch of 100 customers is sent to NetSuite where they are either created as new records or updated if they already exist. A subsidiary relationship record is also created to properly link the customer.

Technical View

The child recipe functions (RF 1.0 US: 54582588, RF 1.0 IE: 54575580) execute a NetSuite batch upsert operation followed by a concurrency wait (custom Ruby sleep), then a bulk create of Customer Subsidiary Relationships. Each operation is logged via data mapper to NetSuite_Integration_Log.

Stage 5: Logging & Audit

Business View

Every batch operation is recorded for audit purposes, including success counts and any errors encountered. This allows the operations team to quickly identify and resolve issues.

Technical View

The child recipes use Workato's logger and data mapper to write structured log entries. Multiple log points capture: (a) upsert results, (b) subsidiary relationship results, (c) error details if any step fails. The recipe has a try/catch block with 0 retries at the top level.

9. Key Business Logic

Subsidiary Classification

Every customer is assigned to either US (Intercom Inc.) or IE (Intercom R&D Unlimited) based on the SUBSIDIARY column in the Snowflake export table. This classification is determined upstream by dbt models and reflects the legal entity that will bill the customer. The recipe does not make this decision — it simply routes customers to the correct NetSuite subsidiary.

Company Name Fallback

When mapping the CompanyName field for NetSuite, the recipe uses a conditional: if customer_name is present, use it; otherwise, fall back to external_id. This ensures every customer record has a name, even if the upstream source did not provide one.

Example: If a Stripe customer has no company name set, the record will be created in NetSuite with the external_id (e.g., "cus_ABC123") as the company name.

Upsert Logic

The NetSuite upsert matches on external_id. If a customer with that external_id already exists in NetSuite, their record is updated with the latest data. If not, a new customer record is created. This means the recipe is idempotent — running it multiple times for the same date will not create duplicates.

Customer Subsidiary Relationships

After upserting the customer record, the child recipes create Customer Subsidiary Relationship records. In NetSuite's OneWorld model, a customer can be associated with multiple subsidiaries. This step ensures the customer is properly linked to their designated subsidiary for billing and financial reporting.

11. Edge Cases & Special Scenarios

Missing Company Name

If a customer has no customer_name in the export table, the recipe falls back to using the external_id as the company name. This prevents NetSuite API errors that would occur with a blank name field, but may result in customer records named with technical identifiers (e.g., "cus_Nk3xQ...").

Empty Subsidiary Batch

If there are no US customers for a given date, the US batch variable will be empty and the US processing branch is skipped entirely (guarded by an if present condition). The same applies to IE. The recipe handles dates with customers in only one subsidiary gracefully.

Large Volume Days

The outer loop handles Snowflake pagination: if a query returns exactly 50,000 rows, the recipe assumes there are more and re-queries with an increased offset. This handles edge cases where bulk migrations or backfills generate thousands of customer records on a single date.

Concurrency / Rate Limiting

The child recipes include a custom Ruby "Concurrency Wait" step between the customer upsert and subsidiary relationship creation. This pause allows NetSuite to finish processing the batch before the next API call, avoiding concurrency errors on the same customer records.

Error Handling

The master recipe wraps all processing in a try/catch block with 0 retries. If an error occurs, the catch block is empty — meaning errors are logged by Workato's job system but the recipe does not attempt automatic recovery. This is by design: customer data issues typically require manual investigation.

Already-Processed Records (Idempotency)

Because the NetSuite operation is an upsert (matched on external_id), re-running the recipe for the same date will update existing records rather than creating duplicates. This makes the recipe safe to re-run during troubleshooting or data backfills.

12. Operational Notes

Deduplication

Deduplication is handled at the NetSuite level via upsert semantics: the external_id field is used as the match key. There is no explicit deduplication in the Workato recipe itself. If the Snowflake export table contains duplicate external_ids for the same date, both will be sent to NetSuite, with the last one processed winning (last-write-wins).

Error Handling Strategy

  • Recipe level: Try/catch with 0 retries — errors are captured but not auto-retried
  • Batch level: If a single customer in a batch of 100 fails the NetSuite upsert, the entire batch may fail depending on NetSuite's bulk API behavior
  • Logging: Multiple log points in child recipes capture success/failure at the upsert stage, subsidiary relationship stage, and overall batch completion
  • Monitoring: Job history shows 27 succeeded, 0 failed out of 29 total (2 were user-cancelled). The recipe is reliable with no organic failures.

Limitations

  • Batch size fixed at 100: Not configurable without modifying the recipe code. May not be optimal for all NetSuite environments.
  • No retry on batch failure: If a batch fails, the remaining batches still process, but the failed batch must be re-run manually.
  • Subsidiary-specific child recipes: US and IE have separate recipes (54582588 and 54575580). Adding a new subsidiary would require creating a new child recipe.
  • Date-based filtering only: The recipe processes all customers for a given created_date. There is no way to filter by specific customer or external_id without modifying the recipe.
  • Child recipes currently stopped: Both RF 1.0 US and IE are in a stopped state. They must be started before the parent recipe can call them.
